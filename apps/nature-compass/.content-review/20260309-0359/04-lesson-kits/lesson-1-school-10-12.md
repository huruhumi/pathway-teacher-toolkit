# Wetland Wonders: Discovering Life at Jinyinhu
**school** | Age 10-12 | 武汉金银湖国家城市湿地公园 (Wuhan Jinyinhu National Urban Wetland Park)

## Roadmap (5 phases)

### ENGAGE: Wetland Welcome & Mystery Sound (0-10 min)
**Activity**: Introduction to Jinyinhu & Sound Immersion

The workshop begins with a warm welcome to Jinyinhu National Urban Wetland Park, emphasizing its unique natural beauty within Wuhan. Students will participate in a mystery sound activity, listening to various wetland sounds like bird calls, frog croaks, and rustling reeds. This activity aims to spark their curiosity and encourage them to guess the environment. Following the sound reveal, a brief discussion will introduce the concept of wetlands as crucial ecosystems, highlighting their role as 'nature's sponges' for water purification and flood prevention. This sets an engaging tone for the day's exploration.

**Steps**:
1. Gather students in a designated safe area and introduce the 'Nature Compass: Wetland Wonders' workshop.
2. Play a 60-second audio recording featuring diverse wetland sounds (e.g., egret calls, frog croaks, water lapping).
3. Ask students: 'What kind of place do you think makes these sounds?' Encourage various guesses.
4. Reveal the answer: 'These are sounds from a wetland, like our amazing Jinyinhu Park!'
5. Briefly explain what a wetland is (land covered by water for part or all of the year) and its importance.
6. Distribute the 'Wetland Wonders' handbooks and direct students to the 'What is a Wetland?' reading page.
7. Encourage students to share one thing they hope to see or learn today at Jinyinhu.

**Background Info**:
- Jinyinhu National Urban Wetland Park, located in Wuhan, Hubei Province, China, spans approximately 11.88 square kilometers, making it one of China's significant urban wetland parks.
- It was established as one of the first national urban wetland parks, aiming to conserve and restore vital wetland ecosystems within a bustling city.
- Wetlands are unique transitional zones, acting as interfaces between dry land and open water, characterized by soils saturated with water.
- These ecosystems are biodiversity hotspots, supporting a rich variety of specialized plants and animals that have adapted to wet conditions.
- Jinyinhu's wetlands provide crucial ecological services to Wuhan, including natural flood control, water purification, and local climate regulation.
- The park is a critical stopover and breeding ground for numerous migratory birds along the East Asian-Australasian Flyway, contributing to global bird conservation.
- Common wetland sounds include the distinctive calls of resident and migratory birds (e.g., herons, kingfishers), the chorus of frogs and toads, and the buzzing of various aquatic insects.

**Teaching Tips**: Use simple, clear language and repeat key terms like 'wetland,' 'habitat,' and 'adaptation' to aid ESL learners. | Foster a welcoming and interactive environment by encouraging student guesses and participation. | If possible, display a large map or pictures of Jinyinhu Park during the introduction to provide visual context. | Ensure the audio recording is played clearly and allow short pauses for students to process and identify sounds. | Prompt students to connect the sounds they hear to potential animals or natural elements, stimulating their imagination.

### EXPLORE: Jinyinhu Species Safari & Sketch (10-35 min)
**Activity**: Guided Observation and Nature Sketching

Students will embark on an exciting 'Species Safari' through a designated, safe area of Jinyinhu Wetland Park. This guided walk focuses on active observation and identification of local wetland species, including plants, birds, insects, and other visible animals. Each student will use their 'Species Safari Log' in the handbook to record their findings, noting physical characteristics, behaviors, and the specific location of their observations. A key component is making a quick sketch of one particularly interesting species, encouraging detailed visual recording. This phase directly engages students with the environment, enhancing their observational and artistic skills.

**Steps**:
1. Explain the 'Species Safari' activity and demonstrate how to effectively use the 'Species Safari Log' in their handbooks.
2. Distribute pencils and clipboards to each student for easy recording during the walk.
3. Lead students on a slow, quiet, and observant walk along a pre-determined, safe path within the wetland.
4. Designate 5-7 minute stops at various points, encouraging silent observation for birds, insects, plants, or animal signs.
5. Guide students to record their observations in their log, including species description, location, and a quick sketch.
6. Facilitate brief small-group sharing sessions where students can discuss their discoveries and sketches.
7. Remind students to maintain a respectful distance from wildlife and to keep noise levels low to avoid disturbance.

**Background Info**:
- Jinyinhu Wetland Park is a haven for over 100 bird species, featuring common residents such as the elegant Little Egret, the stately Grey Heron, and various species of ducks.
- The park's diverse flora, including extensive reed beds (Phragmites australis) and beautiful water lilies (Nymphaea spp.), provides essential shelter, nesting sites, and food sources for countless animals.
- The presence of numerous dragonflies and damselflies indicates good water quality and highlights their role as both predators and prey within the wetland food web.
- Observing animal behaviors, such as a bird foraging for food or an insect resting on a leaf, provides valuable insights into their daily lives and ecological roles.
- Nature sketching is a powerful tool for enhancing observational skills, compelling students to pay close attention to intricate details like shape, color, texture, and proportion.
- Different micro-habitats within the wetland, such as shallow water, dense vegetation, or open mudflats, support distinct communities of species due to varying environmental conditions and resource availability.
- The diversity and health of species, particularly indicator species like amphibians or certain fish, serve as important benchmarks for assessing the overall ecological health of the wetland ecosystem.

**Teaching Tips**: Strongly emphasize the importance of quiet observation to minimize disturbance to wildlife and maximize viewing opportunities. | Provide clear and simple examples of what to look for, such as 'Look for birds with long, thin legs near the water's edge' or 'Can you spot a dragonfly resting on a reed?' | Use simplified vocabulary for common species names, or prepare visual identification cards to assist students. | Actively circulate among students, offering guidance and encouragement for their sketching and note-taking. | Remind students to engage their senses (sight, sound, smell) but to avoid touching any unfamiliar plants or animals for safety.

### EXPLAIN: Adaptations & Ecosystems Unveiled (35-55 min)
**Activity**: Discussion on Wetland Adaptations and Food Chains

This phase delves into the fascinating world of biological adaptations, explaining how wetland species are uniquely suited to their environment. Through a guided discussion, students will learn about specific physical and behavioral traits that enable plants and animals to survive and thrive in Jinyinhu's wet conditions. Concepts like 'habitat' and basic 'food chains' will be introduced, illustrating how different species interact and depend on each other. The teacher will use examples from the 'Meet Jinyinhu's Residents' and 'Amazing Adaptations' handbook pages to concretize these ecological principles, fostering a deeper understanding of biodiversity.

**Steps**:
1. Gather students in a comfortable, shaded spot, ideally with a view of some wetland vegetation.
2. Initiate a discussion by asking students to share some of their most interesting observations from the 'Species Safari'.
3. Introduce the concept of 'adaptation' using simple language and relatable examples (e.g., 'Why do different birds have different kinds of beaks?').
4. Discuss specific adaptations of Jinyinhu species: egrets' long legs for wading, ducks' webbed feet for swimming, reeds' hollow stems for oxygen transport to roots.
5. Explain the concept of 'habitat' and how Jinyinhu offers diverse habitats for various species.
6. Introduce basic food chain principles, illustrating 'who eats whom' with examples from Jinyinhu (e.g., insects eat plants, fish eat insects, birds eat fish).
7. Direct students to the 'Amazing Adaptations' reading and 'Habitat Detective' activity in their handbooks for further exploration.

**Background Info**:
- **Adaptations** are specialized features or behaviors that have evolved over time, enabling an organism to survive and reproduce successfully in its particular environment.
- **Egrets and Herons** possess remarkably long legs, allowing them to wade through shallow waters without getting their bodies wet, and sharp, spear-like beaks perfectly suited for catching fish and amphibians.
- **Ducks** are characterized by their webbed feet, which provide efficient propulsion for swimming, and their flattened bills, designed for sifting small organisms and plant matter from water or mud.
- **Reeds (Phragmites australis)** have evolved hollow stems (aerenchyma) that facilitate the transport of oxygen to their roots, which are often submerged in anaerobic (oxygen-poor) waterlogged soil.
- **Water Lilies** feature broad, flat leaves that float on the water surface, maximizing their exposure to sunlight for photosynthesis, and long, flexible stems that anchor them to the muddy bottom.
- A **habitat** is the natural home or environment where an organism lives, providing all necessary resources like food, water, shelter, and space; wetlands offer a mosaic of diverse habitats.
- A **food chain** illustrates the linear transfer of energy from one living organism to another through feeding, starting with producers and moving through various levels of consumers.
- An **ecosystem** is a complex biological community of interacting organisms and their non-living physical environment, with the Jinyinhu wetland serving as a prime example of such a dynamic system.

**Teaching Tips**: Utilize visual aids such as large pictures of wetland animals and plants, highlighting their specific adaptations. | Pose open-ended questions to stimulate critical thinking, such as 'Why do you think this bird has such a long neck?' | Draw simple food chains on a portable whiteboard or in the dirt to visually explain energy flow. | Continuously link the discussion of adaptations back to the actual species students observed during their safari. | Ensure new vocabulary (e.g., 'adaptation,' 'habitat,' 'food chain') is clearly defined and reinforced through repetition.

### ELABORATE: Wetland Web & Community Connection (55-75 min)
**Activity**: Food Web Construction and Discussion on Wetland Services

Building upon the understanding of individual species and their adaptations, this phase expands to the broader wetland ecosystem and its profound importance to the local community. Students will engage in a hands-on activity to construct a simplified 'Wetland Food Web' using cards depicting Jinyinhu species and string to illustrate interdependencies. Following this, a guided discussion will highlight how Jinyinhu's wetland provides essential ecosystem services to Wuhan residents, such as natural water purification, flood protection, and recreational opportunities. This segment integrates social science, demonstrating the direct relevance of wetland health to human well-being.

**Steps**:
1. Review the concept of a food chain and introduce the more complex idea of a 'food web' (many interconnected food chains).
2. Divide students into small groups and provide each group with pre-made cards of Jinyinhu species (plants, insects, fish, birds) and lengths of string.
3. Instruct groups to connect the species cards with string to visually represent 'who eats whom,' forming their own basic food web.
4. Facilitate a brief sharing session where each group presents and explains their constructed food web.
5. Transition the discussion to the broader importance of wetlands for human communities, covering topics like clean water, flood control, and recreation.
6. Ask students: 'Based on what we've learned, how does Jinyinhu specifically help the people living in Wuhan?'
7. Direct students to the 'Wetlands and Us: A Special Connection' reading and 'The Jinyinhu Food Web' background knowledge in their handbooks.

**Background Info**:
- A **food web** is a complex, interconnected network of food chains within an ecosystem, illustrating the intricate feeding relationships and the flow of energy among various organisms.
- **Ecosystem services** are the myriad direct and indirect benefits that humans derive from the natural environment and the healthy functioning of ecosystems.
- **Water Purification:** Wetlands function as natural biological filters, effectively removing sediments, excess nutrients, and various pollutants from water as it slowly passes through their vegetation and soils, significantly improving water quality.
- **Flood Control:** The dense vegetation and saturated soils of wetlands act as natural sponges, absorbing and temporarily storing large volumes of rainwater, thereby mitigating flood risks and reducing their severity in urbanized areas like Wuhan.
- **Biodiversity Hotspot:** Wetlands are globally recognized for their exceptional biodiversity, supporting a disproportionately high number of species and contributing significantly to global genetic diversity.
- **Recreation and Tourism:** Jinyinhu provides invaluable recreational opportunities for Wuhan's residents, offering spaces for birdwatching, walking, cycling, and quiet contemplation, which enhance public health and well-being.
- **Cultural Importance:** Beyond their ecological functions, wetlands often hold deep cultural significance, serving as sources of inspiration for art, literature, and traditional practices within local communities.
- **Local Economy:** Sustainable management of wetlands can foster ecotourism and provide opportunities for local businesses, contributing positively to the regional economy.

**Teaching Tips**: Prepare species cards with clear images and simple names (English and Chinese if appropriate) to facilitate the food web activity. | Actively guide groups during the food web construction, ensuring they understand the directional flow of energy (arrows from eaten to eater). | Use relatable analogies for ecosystem services, such as 'Imagine if there were no wetlands to clean our water; what would that mean for us?' | Encourage students to make personal connections by asking if they or their families have visited Jinyinhu and what they enjoy doing there. | Emphasize the critical message that protecting wetlands is a win-win situation, benefiting both natural ecosystems and human societies.

### EVALUATE: My Wetland Promise & Sharing (75-90 min)
**Activity**: Personal Commitment and Reflection

The final phase encourages students to reflect on their learning journey and make a personal commitment to wetland conservation. Students will complete the 'My Wetland Promise' activity in their handbook, where they will write or draw one specific action they can take to help protect Jinyinhu or other wetlands. This activity reinforces the importance of individual responsibility and empowers them to become active stewards of the environment. Volunteers will then have the opportunity to share their promises with the group, fostering a sense of collective commitment. The workshop concludes with a summary of key takeaways and the distribution of 'Wetland Explorer' certificates.

**Steps**:
1. Begin by asking students to recall their favorite observation or the most surprising fact they learned during the workshop.
2. Introduce the 'My Wetland Promise' activity located in their handbooks, explaining its purpose as a personal commitment.
3. Clearly explain that a 'promise' is a serious commitment to take a specific action, big or small, to help wetlands.
4. Allocate 5-7 minutes for students to complete their promise, encouraging both written descriptions and illustrative drawings.
5. Invite volunteers to share their 'Wetland Promises' with the entire group, fostering a sense of shared responsibility.
6. Conclude the workshop with a brief summary of the key takeaways: the vitality of wetlands, their rich biodiversity, and their importance to people.
7. Distribute 'Wetland Explorer' certificates to all participants, thanking them for their engagement and contributions.

**Background Info**:
- **Conservation** refers to the careful preservation and protection of something, especially a planned management of a natural resource to prevent exploitation, destruction, or neglect.
- **Individual Action:** The cumulative effect of many small, conscious actions taken by individuals can lead to significant positive impacts on environmental protection and sustainability.
- **Examples of Promises:** Simple, actionable commitments include not littering in natural areas, conserving water at home, learning more about local wildlife, sharing wetland knowledge with family and friends, or participating in community clean-up events.
- **Community Engagement:** Encouraging students to share their newfound knowledge and commitments with their families and peers helps to broaden awareness and cultivate a collective sense of environmental stewardship.
- **Stewardship:** This concept involves taking responsible care of something, particularly the environment, with the understanding that it is a shared resource to be preserved for future generations.
- **Reflection:** The act of reflecting on learned material helps students consolidate their knowledge, make personal connections to the topic, and internalize the importance of environmental issues.
- **Empowerment:** Providing students with a platform to voice their commitments and a sense of agency in environmental protection can inspire a lifelong dedication to conservation efforts.

**Teaching Tips**: Offer a few simple, achievable examples of promises to inspire students and make the task less daunting. | Ensure that all students feel comfortable and supported, but do not pressure anyone to share their promise if they prefer not to. | Reinforce the idea that every action, no matter how small, contributes positively to wetland conservation. | End the workshop on an uplifting and empowering note, expressing genuine appreciation for the students' efforts and enthusiasm. | Have the 'Wetland Explorer' certificates pre-filled or ready for quick distribution to ensure a smooth and celebratory conclusion.

### P5: Reading: What is a Wetland? [ENGAGE - Background Information] (364 words)
Imagine a special place where land and water meet, creating a unique environment. This place is called a **wetland**. Wetlands are areas of land that are covered by water, either all the time or for a long part of the year. They are not deep like a lake or ocean, but rather shallow, allowing many plants to grow their roots in the waterlogged soil. Jinyinhu National Urban Wetland Park in Wuhan is a wonderful example of such a place, right in our city! It's a natural treasure that combines the beauty of nature with the urban landscape. Wetlands are sometimes called 'nature's kidneys' because they help clean water, just like our kidneys clean our bodies. They filter out pollution and harmful substances, making the water clearer and healthier for everyone. This important job helps keep the air fresh and the environment balanced. Without wetlands, our water would be much dirtier, and many plants and animals would lose their homes. The plants and animals that live in wetlands are very special. They have learned to live in wet conditions. For example, some plants have roots that can breathe underwater, and some birds have long legs to walk in shallow water. These amazing **adaptations** help them survive and thrive. Jinyinhu is home to many such creatures, from tiny insects to beautiful birds. We will discover some of them today! Wetlands also act like giant sponges. When there is a lot of rain, they soak up the extra water. This helps prevent floods in nearby towns and cities. Later, when it's dry, the wetland slowly releases this water, keeping the land around it moist. This natural process is incredibly important for the local community, protecting homes and farms from too much or too little water. This makes wetlands vital for both nature and people. **Vocabulary:** Wetland: An area of land covered by water. Adaptations: Special features that help living things survive. Filter: To remove unwanted substances from a liquid or gas. **Think About It:** 1. What is one way wetlands are like 'nature's kidneys'? 2. Why are wetlands important for preventing floods? 3. Can you name one type of living thing you might find in a wetland?
