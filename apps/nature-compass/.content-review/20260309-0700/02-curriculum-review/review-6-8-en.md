Here's a review of the STEAM outdoor curriculum from the two expert perspectives, followed by specific, actionable feedback.

---

## Perspective 1: ESL Teacher (CEFR A1)

**Evaluation:**
The curriculum has a commendable intention to integrate English language learning with STEAM outdoor exploration. The use of simple sentence structures ("I see a [color] [plant].") and action verbs ("build," "pour," "stop") is excellent for A1 learners. The idea of a song for body parts and adjectives for movement is also well-aligned with this level.

However, a significant challenge lies in the vocabulary chosen for the themes and some of the goals. Many terms are above CEFR A1 for 6-8 year olds. Words like "buoyancy," "migratory," "species," "habitats," "thorax," "abdomen," "filtration," and "differentiate" are likely to be too complex without extensive pre-teaching and visual aids, potentially overwhelming young learners and hindering their comprehension of the STEAM concepts. While the *activities* are hands-on and engaging, the *language* used to frame them and the specific vocabulary targets often miss the A1 mark. Instructions are generally clear for the activities themselves, but the conceptual language around them would require significant simplification. There are missed opportunities for more structured language practice beyond the stated goals, such as simple Q&A, repetitive chants, or labeling activities with simpler terms.

**Score: 6/10**
The curriculum has a good foundation for ESL integration with appropriate activity types, but the vocabulary level is consistently too high for CEFR A1 young learners, which would impede effective learning and participation. More explicit scaffolding and simplification are needed.

## Perspective 2: Activity Planner / 研学设计师

**Evaluation:**
This curriculum demonstrates a strong understanding of suitable locations in Wuhan and age-appropriate outdoor activities. The chosen parks (East Lake, Jiefang Park, Sha Lake, Hankou Jiangtan) are all real, accessible, and excellent choices for wetland exploration, offering diverse environments for students aged 6-8.

The activity design is largely age-appropriate, focusing on hands-on discovery, sensory engagement (mud kitchen, touching soil), and active participation (scavenger hunt, movement game, building). The cognitive load for activities like identifying plants/insects, simple experiments, and building barriers is well-matched for this age group. "Mapping bird homes" might be slightly abstract without a very clear, simplified template, but it's manageable. The physical demands are well-balanced, incorporating walking, observation, and fine motor skills.

The 4 lessons form a coherent ecological progression, moving from basic plant life to animals, insects, and finally the abiotic factors (soil, water) and human interaction. This provides a holistic view of the wetland ecosystem.

A critical omission is the complete lack of indoor alternatives. For an outdoor curriculum in Wuhan, where weather conditions (rain, extreme heat/cold, air quality) can be unpredictable, having genuinely equivalent indoor alternatives is essential for program continuity and safety. This is a significant flaw.

Regarding the 180-minute duration per lesson: while the activities are rich enough to fill this time, 3 hours is a long stretch for 6-8 year olds. It will require very careful planning with built-in breaks, snack times, and varied pacing to maintain engagement and prevent fatigue. The curriculum doesn't explicitly detail this structure.

**Score: 7/10**
The curriculum excels in location selection, age-appropriate activity design, and ecological coherence. However, the complete absence of indoor alternatives is a major practical oversight, and the 180-minute lesson duration requires more detailed structuring to be realistic for young learners.

## Top 5 Specific Improvements

1.  **Integrate Comprehensive Indoor Alternatives for Each Lesson:** This is the most critical improvement. For each outdoor lesson, design a specific, engaging indoor alternative that maintains the core STEAM learning objectives. These should be detailed with materials, activities, and learning outcomes, ensuring the program can run effectively regardless of weather conditions in Wuhan.
2.  **Simplify English Vocabulary and Enhance Language Scaffolding:** Review all thematic vocabulary (e.g., "buoyancy," "migratory," "thorax," "filtration") and replace them with simpler A1-level terms or provide explicit, visual-based explanations. Incorporate more structured language practice such as:
    *   Pre-teaching key vocabulary with flashcards and realia.
    *   Simple Q&A sessions (e.g., "What do you see?", "How many?").
    *   Repetitive chants or songs for concepts beyond just insect parts.
    *   Sentence frames for observations (e.g., "The [plant] is [color]. It [floats/sinks].").
3.  **Structure the 180-Minute Lessons with Explicit Pacing and Breaks:** Provide a detailed breakdown of how each 180-minute lesson will be managed. This should include:
    *   Welcome/briefing (15 min)
    *   Activity Block 1 (45-60 min)
    *   Snack/Rest/Bathroom Break (15-20 min)
    *   Activity Block 2 (45-60 min)
    *   Reflection/Journaling/Sharing (15-20 min)
    *   Wrap-up/Dismissal (10 min)
    This ensures realistic pacing and sustained engagement for 6-8 year olds.
4.  **Enhance Explicit STEAM Integration and Progression:** While STEAM elements are listed, make the *how* more explicit. For example, for "Science," specify the scientific method steps being used. For "Engineering," highlight the design and problem-solving process. Consider a clearer progression of STEAM skills across the four lessons (e.g., introducing basic measurement/data collection (Math) earlier, or building on simple engineering design principles).
5.  **Develop Pre- and Post-Visit Activities:** To maximize learning impact, include short, engaging activities for before and after each outdoor lesson.
    *   **Pre-visit:** Introduce key vocabulary, discuss safety rules, set expectations.
    *   **Post-visit:** Encourage drawing, writing simple sentences about observations, sharing experiences, or completing a simple worksheet to reinforce learning and connect the outdoor experience to classroom concepts.
