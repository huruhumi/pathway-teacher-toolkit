# Nature Compass: An Autumn Explorer's Mission
**family** | Age 10-12

## Roadmap (6 phases)

### ENGAGE (0-20 min)
**Activity**: Mission Briefing & Map Skills [Science/Technology/Math]

Begin your adventure by becoming official Nature Navigators. In this first phase, you and your child will receive your mission briefing and a map of High Line Park. The goal is to spark curiosity about navigation and mapping in a natural context. You'll work together to identify key landmarks on the map, such as specific art installations or garden zones. Then, you'll introduce a simple compass—either a real one or a smartphone app—to find North. This initial activity connects the abstract concept of a map to the physical world around you, setting the stage for a day of directed exploration and observation.

**Steps**:
1. Gather in a circle and present the 'Mission Briefing' to the children, building excitement for being 'Nature Navigators'.
2. Distribute the park map and the 'Explorer Kit' to each family.
3. Guide your child to find your current location ('You Are Here') on the map. Point to a destination for your next activity.
4. Introduce the compass. If using a smartphone app, open it together. Explain that the red arrow always points North.
5. Help your child turn their body and the map so they are both facing North. This is called 'orienting the map'.
6. Practice finding North, South, East, and West in your immediate surroundings.
7. Ask your child to predict which direction you will walk to get to the first exploration spot.

**Background Info**:
- A compass works because of Earth's magnetism. The Earth acts like a giant bar magnet with a North and South pole.
- The needle of a compass is a lightweight magnet. The Earth's magnetic field pulls on the needle, causing it to align with the North-South line.
- Before compasses, sailors navigated using the sun's position (it rises in the East and sets in the West) and stars, like the North Star (Polaris).
- The High Line is a 1.45-mile-long elevated park built on a former New York Central Railroad spur. It's an example of 'rewilding' an urban structure.
- Maps use a 'scale' to represent large distances in a small space. For example, one inch on the map might equal 100 feet in the park.
- Cardinal directions (North, East, South, West) are the four main points of a compass. Intermediate directions (like Northeast) are between them.

**Teaching Tips**: For Parents: Use simple analogies. Explain Earth's magnetism like two magnets snapping together. 'The Earth has a big invisible magnet inside, and our little compass needle wants to point to it!' | ESL Scaffolding: Use the sentence frame: 'I see the [landmark] on the map. It is to the [direction].' Point and repeat the words 'North', 'South', 'East', 'West' clearly. | Outdoor Management: Establish a 'home base' spot. Use a simple call-and-response signal (e.g., Parent: 'Nature Navigators!', Child: 'Ready to Explore!') to get attention. | Differentiation: For children who grasp it quickly, ask them to find intermediate directions like Northwest. For those who need support, focus only on finding North.

**Phase Supplies**: Printed map of High Line Park, Magnetic compass or smartphone with a compass app, Explorer Handbook

**Conversation**: If we didn't have a map or a compass, how else could we find our way? | What do you think the different colors and symbols on the map mean? | Why do you think it's important for the compass needle to always point North?

### EXPLORE (Part 1) (20-60 min)
**Activity**: Autumn Leaf Collection & Analysis [Science/Art/Math]

Now that you're oriented, it's time for hands-on science. This phase is a sensory exploration of autumn's most visible sign: changing leaves. Guide your child on a 'leaf hunt' to find as many different shapes, colors, and sizes as they can. This is not just about collecting; it's about careful observation. Encourage them to use their magnifying glass to look at the veins and textures. They will use their handbook's checklist to categorize what they find, turning a simple walk into a data collection exercise. This activity hones observation skills and introduces basic botanical concepts in a tangible way.

**Steps**:
1. Lead the group to a safe, designated area with plenty of fallen leaves.
2. Explain the 'look but don't pick' rule for leaves still on trees. We are only collecting leaves that have already fallen.
3. Give your child a collection bag and a magnifying glass. Challenge them to find at least five different kinds of leaves.
4. As they find leaves, encourage them to describe what they see. 'Look at this one! It's bright red and has pointy edges.'
5. Use the 'Leaf Detective Checklist' in the handbook. Help them identify the shape (e.g., oval, star, needle) and color.
6. Use a small ruler or their hand to measure/estimate the size of their favorite leaf and record it.
7. Group Management: Remind children to stay within the designated rope/cone boundaries and always be able to see their parent.

**Background Info**:
- Leaves get their green color from a chemical called chlorophyll. Chlorophyll helps plants make food using sunlight (photosynthesis).
- In autumn, as days get shorter and colder, trees stop making food. The chlorophyll breaks down, revealing other colors that were there all along, like yellow (xanthophyll) and orange (carotene).
- Red and purple colors are often caused by anthocyanins, pigments that are produced in the autumn in response to bright light and cold temperatures.
- The veins in a leaf are like tiny pipes that carry water from the stem to the rest of the leaf.
- The shape of a leaf helps us identify the tree. For example, Maple leaves are star-shaped, while Oak leaves have rounded lobes.
- Trees drop their leaves in winter to conserve water and energy. Without leaves, they don't lose as much water to the dry winter air.

**Teaching Tips**: For Parents: Explain chlorophyll like a green mask. 'In summer, the leaf wears a green mask called chlorophyll. In autumn, it takes the mask off, and we see its true yellow and orange face!' | ESL Scaffolding: Provide sentence frames: 'I found a ____ (color) leaf.' 'This leaf is ____ (shape/size).' Use gestures to explain words like 'pointy,' 'smooth,' and 'bumpy.' | Tool Handling: Show your child how to hold the magnifying glass close to their eye and move the leaf back and forth to get it in focus. | Differentiation: Challenge advanced learners to find leaves with signs of insect activity (like chewed holes). Support others by focusing on just finding two different colors.

**Phase Supplies**: Collection bag (reusable cloth or paper), Magnifying glass, Explorer Handbook and pencil, Small ruler

**Conversation**: Why do you think all the leaves aren't the same shape? | What does this leaf feel like? How is it different from that one? | I wonder what this leaf looked like in the summer.

### EXPLORE (Part 2) (60-90 min)
**Activity**: The Soil Story [Science]

After looking up at the trees, it's time to look down at the ground beneath our feet. In this phase, you and your child will become 'Soil Scientists.' You'll find a safe spot to examine the soil up close. Using a spoon or a stick, you will gently scoop a small sample onto a paper plate to observe. What is it made of? With a magnifying glass, you can discover a hidden world of tiny pebbles, decaying leaves (humus), and maybe even a wiggly worm or a tiny insect. This activity reveals that soil is not just 'dirt,' but a complex, living ecosystem essential for the plants you've been admiring.

**Steps**:
1. Find a designated soil exploration area. Remind your child we are just looking and will return the soil when we are done.
2. Lay down a piece of paper or a paper plate to create a 'lab station'.
3. Using a craft stick or spoon, guide your child to scoop a small amount of topsoil onto the plate.
4. Ask: 'What do you see?' Encourage them to use the magnifying glass to look for different components.
5. Help them sort the soil into piles: tiny rocks, dark soft bits (humus), sticks, and leaves.
6. If you find any living creatures like worms or beetles, observe them gently and talk about their role in the soil.
7. Before leaving, carefully return the soil sample to where you found it and wash hands thoroughly with sanitizer.

**Background Info**:
- Soil is made of four main ingredients: minerals (from broken-down rock), organic matter (decaying plants and animals, called humus), water, and air.
- Humus is the dark, nutrient-rich part of soil that acts like a sponge, holding water and providing food for plants.
- A single teaspoon of healthy soil can contain billions of microscopic organisms like bacteria and fungi.
- Earthworms are 'soil engineers.' As they burrow, they create tunnels that let air and water in, and their castings (poop) enrich the soil.
- Soil forms in layers called 'horizons.' The topsoil (the 'A horizon') is the darkest and has the most life and nutrients.
- The texture of soil depends on the size of its mineral particles: sand (largest), silt (medium), and clay (smallest).

**Teaching Tips**: For Parents: Explain soil components like a cake recipe. 'Soil is like a cake! It needs flour (minerals), sugar and eggs (organic matter), and milk (water) to be good for the plants.' | ESL Scaffolding: Use simple, descriptive words. 'The rock is hard.' 'The soil is soft.' 'The worm is wiggly.' Use the sentence frame: 'In the soil, I see ____.' | Safety: Emphasize the 'look but don't touch' rule for unknown insects and the importance of hand washing after handling soil. Ensure no one puts their hands near their mouth. | Differentiation: For older children, ask them to draw the different components they see in their handbook. For younger children, focus on the tactile experience of feeling the different textures.

**Phase Supplies**: Magnifying glass, Craft sticks or old spoons, Paper plate or piece of white paper, Hand sanitizer

**Conversation**: Why do you think soil is so important for the trees and plants? | What do you think it would be like to be an earthworm living in the soil? | How does this soil feel different from sand at the beach?

### EXPLAIN (90-120 min)
**Activity**: Nature's Clues Debrief [Science]

After exploring, it's time to connect the dots. Find a comfortable spot to sit together and share your discoveries. This is the 'EXPLAIN' phase, where you help your child make sense of what they've seen. You'll look at the leaves and soil samples you collected and discuss the 'why' behind your observations. Why are the leaves red and not green? What does soil do? As the parent-guide, you can share some of the fascinating 'Did You Know?' facts from your background info, transforming observations into deeper scientific understanding. This conversational phase helps solidify new vocabulary and concepts in a relaxed, social setting.

**Steps**:
1. Gather the group in a circle with the collected leaves and soil observation plates.
2. Ask each child to choose their favorite leaf and describe it to the group using the sentence frames they practiced.
3. Pose guiding questions: 'What did we notice about all the leaves we found?' 'What colors did we see most often?'
4. Share a simplified explanation for why leaves change color (the 'green mask' analogy).
5. Discuss the soil observations. Ask, 'Was the soil just dirt? What else did we find?'
6. Explain the importance of soil for plant life using the 'cake recipe' or 'plant food' analogy.
7. Encourage questions and celebrate every child's contribution to the discussion.

**Background Info**:
- The process of leaves falling is called 'abscission.' The tree forms a special layer of cells at the base of the leaf stem that pushes it off.
- Decomposition is the process where organic matter (like fallen leaves) is broken down by bacteria, fungi, and worms, turning it into nutrient-rich humus.
- A deciduous tree is one that loses its leaves annually (like oak and maple). An evergreen tree (like a pine) keeps its needles year-round.
- The High Line's soil is specially engineered to be lightweight and drain well, as it sits on an elevated structure. It's not the same as the soil you'd find in a forest.
- Sunlight is a key trigger for autumn colors. Sunny days and cool nights in autumn lead to the most vibrant red and purple leaf colors.
- Many animals rely on the layer of fallen leaves (leaf litter) for camouflage, food, and shelter during the winter.

**Teaching Tips**: For Parents: Your role here is to be a 'curiosity guide,' not a lecturer. Start with 'I wonder...' or 'Did you know...?' to share facts in an engaging way. | ESL Scaffolding: Create a 'word wall' on a large piece of paper with the new vocabulary (Chlorophyll, Humus, Decompose). Draw simple pictures next to each word. | Group Management: Use a 'talking stick' or another object; only the person holding it can speak. This ensures everyone gets a turn. | Connect to Personal Experience: Ask questions like, 'Do the trees near our home look like this in autumn?' 'What does the soil in our garden look like?'

**Phase Supplies**: Collected leaf specimens, Soil observation plates, Explorer Handbook for reference, Large paper and markers for 'word wall' (optional)

**Conversation**: Which discovery surprised you the most today, the leaves or the soil? | If you were a tree, what color would you want your leaves to be in the fall? | What do you think happens to all these leaves after they fall on the ground?

### ELABORATE (120-160 min)
**Activity**: Build a Nature Compass [Engineering/Art/Science]

Now it's time to apply everything you've learned in a creative, hands-on challenge. In this phase, your family will use the natural materials you've collected—leaves, twigs, pebbles, acorns—to build a large compass on the ground. First, you'll use your real compass to find North again. Then, you'll design and construct your own. This is an engineering and art challenge: you must correctly orient the cardinal directions (N, E, S, W) and then decorate each direction with different natural items. For example, you could use red leaves for East (sunrise) and dark twigs for North. This activity synthesizes navigation skills, scientific observation, and artistic expression.

**Steps**:
1. Lead the group to a flat, open space where they can build without disturbing plants.
2. Demonstrate how to find North one more time with the compass.
3. Instruct each family to find a space and lay down two long sticks in a cross shape, with one stick pointing North-South and the other East-West.
4. Challenge them to mark each direction. They can make the letters N, E, S, W out of small twigs or pebbles.
5. Encourage creativity: 'Let's use our most colorful leaves to mark East, where the sun rises!' or 'Let's use dark stones to mark North.'
6. Guide your child in arranging the materials. This is a great opportunity for collaboration and design thinking.
7. Walk around and admire the different compass designs, asking children to explain their creative choices.

**Background Info**:
- Many ancient cultures created large-scale constructions related to cardinal directions and astronomy, like Stonehenge, which aligns with the sunrise on the summer solstice.
- In many cultures, the four directions have symbolic meanings. For example, in some Native American traditions, each direction is associated with a specific spirit, color, and season.
- This type of art, made from natural materials and left in the environment, is called 'Land Art' or 'Earth Art.' A famous artist who does this is Andy Goldsworthy.
- The sun's path can be used as a natural compass. In the Northern Hemisphere, shadows move clockwise. A stick in the ground will cast its shortest shadow at noon, and that shadow will point North.
- Moss often (but not always!) grows more thickly on the northern side of trees in the Northern Hemisphere, as it receives less direct sunlight and stays damper.

**Teaching Tips**: For Parents: Focus on the process, not the perfect product. Praise your child's effort and creative ideas. 'What a clever way to make the letter S!' | ESL Scaffolding: Practice the direction words again. As they place items, say 'You are putting the red leaf in the East.' Have them repeat. Use prepositions: 'Put the stone on the line. Put the leaf next to the twig.' | Environmental Responsibility: Remind everyone that this is temporary art. We will leave our materials where they are to decompose naturally and not disturb any living plants. | Differentiation: Younger children can focus on sorting materials by color for each direction. Older children can be challenged to add intermediate directions (NE, SW, etc.).

**Phase Supplies**: All collected natural materials (leaves, twigs, pebbles), Magnetic compass or smartphone app, Long sticks (at least 4 per group)

**Conversation**: If we came back tomorrow, what do you think our compass would look like? | Besides a compass, what other things in nature could help us know which way is North? | What part of your design are you most proud of? Why?

### EVALUATE (160-180 min)
**Activity**: Explorer's Showcase & Reflection [Science]

In the final phase of our mission, we celebrate our discoveries. Each family will stand by their completed Nature Compass and present it to the rest of the group. This is the 'Explorer's Showcase.' Your child can share which direction they decorated and why they chose those specific materials. It’s a chance to practice their new vocabulary and share what they learned in a proud, supportive environment. We'll conclude by reflecting on the day's single most exciting discovery. This final sharing circle reinforces the learning, builds confidence in communication, and ends the workshop with a sense of accomplishment and community.

**Steps**:
1. Gather the group for a 'gallery walk' to see all the different nature compasses.
2. Give each family 1-2 minutes to present their compass. Prompt your child: 'Tell us about the East on your compass. What did you use?'
3. Give a big round of applause for each family's creation.
4. Take photos of the children with their compasses.
5. Gather for a final circle. Ask the reflection question: 'What was the most amazing thing you discovered today?'
6. Present each child with their 'Official Nature Explorer' certificate from the handbook.
7. Thank everyone for being wonderful scientists and officially conclude the mission.

**Background Info**:
- Scientific communication is a critical skill for scientists. They share their findings through papers, conferences, and presentations so others can learn from their work.
- Reflection is a key part of the learning process. It helps transfer knowledge from short-term to long-term memory.
- Portfolio-based assessment (like showing a project) is a common way to evaluate understanding in hands-on learning, as it focuses on what the student can *do*.
- Nature journaling, a practice started by naturalists like John Muir and Charles Darwin, is a way of combining scientific observation, drawing, and personal reflection.
- Celebrating achievements, even small ones, builds intrinsic motivation and a positive attitude towards learning.
- Group sharing helps build a 'community of practice,' where learners feel safe to share ideas and learn from one another.

**Teaching Tips**: For Parents: Help your child prepare one or two sentences for their presentation. If they are shy, you can present together. 'We decided to use...'  | ESL Scaffolding: Model the reflection sentence first: 'My favorite discovery was the wiggly worm in the soil.' Then invite your child to use the frame: 'My favorite discovery was ____.' | Positive Reinforcement: Focus on praising effort, creativity, and teamwork. 'I love how carefully you placed all those tiny pebbles.' 'You worked so well together!' | Leave No Trace: After photos and sharing, gently disperse the compass materials nearby to leave the area as you found it, explaining why this is important for the park.

**Phase Supplies**: Completed Nature Compasses, Explorer Handbook with certificate page, Camera or smartphone for photos

**Conversation**: What was your favorite part of our mission today? | What is one new word you learned today that you want to remember? | Where should our next nature exploration mission be?
