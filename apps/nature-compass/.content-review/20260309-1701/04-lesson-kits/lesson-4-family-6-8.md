# Nature Compass: Wetland Explorer!
**family** | Age 6-8

## Roadmap (8 phases)

### PRE-CLASS MISSION (Pre-Workshop)
**Activity**: Explorer Disguise Craft [Art]

Let's get ready for our big adventure! Before we go to the wetlands, every great explorer needs a special piece of gear. You and your child will create your very own 'Nature Explorer' vest from a simple paper bag. This is a fun way to get excited and talk about what you might see. Decorate it with pictures of ducks, leaves, and bugs you hope to find. This simple craft builds anticipation and introduces the theme in a playful, hands-on way.

**Steps**:
1. Find a large paper grocery bag. This will be your vest!
2. Help your child cut a hole for their head and two holes for their arms.
3. Lay the vest flat and get out your crayons, markers, or stickers.
4. Talk about the animals we might see. 'Should we draw a duck? Or a little frog?'
5. Encourage your child to draw leaves, bugs, and birds all over their vest.
6. Let them try it on! They are now an official Nature Explorer!
7. Pack the vest to wear during our workshop adventure.

**Background Info**:
- Crafting helps develop fine motor skills, which are important for writing and drawing.
- Talking about the animals and plants you might see is a great way to introduce new vocabulary before the trip.
- Creating a 'uniform' or 'costume' can make the experience feel more special and immersive for a child.
- This activity connects the home environment to the upcoming learning experience, making the child feel more prepared.
- The paper bag is a recyclable material, subtly introducing the concept of using resources wisely.

**Teaching Tips**: Use simple sentences: 'Let's draw a duck.' 'What color is the leaf?' | Praise their creativity: 'Wow, what a beautiful drawing!' 'You are a great artist!' | Ask 'What is this?' to encourage them to name the things they are drawing in English. | Model the language. Point to a picture and say, 'This is a green frog.' Then ask, 'What color is the frog?'

**Phase Supplies**: Large paper grocery bag, Child-safe scissors, Crayons or markers, Stickers (optional)

**Conversation**: What animal are you most excited to see at the wetland? | What colors do you think we will find in nature today? | If you were a duck, where would you swim?

### 1. ENGAGE (0-20 mins)
**Activity**: Wetland Welcome & Sensory Warm-up [Science]

Welcome to the wetlands! 🦆 It's time to put on our Explorer Vests and get our senses ready. We'll start with a fun welcome circle where we greet each other and the nature around us. Then, we'll play a 'Super Senses' game. What can you hear? What can you see? What can you smell? This short, exciting start grabs your child's attention, introduces the environment in a safe, structured way, and prepares their minds and bodies for the exploration ahead. We are tuning into nature's radio station!

**Steps**:
1. Find a nice, open spot near the entrance. Sit in a circle together.
2. Lead a simple welcome song or chant, like 'Hello nature, here we come!'
3. Guide your child to close their eyes for 10 seconds. Ask, 'What sounds can you hear? A bird? The wind?'
4. Now, open your eyes. 'What is the biggest thing you can see? What is the smallest?'
5. Take a gentle sniff of the air. 'Does it smell like flowers? Or wet leaves?'
6. Use the sentence frame 'I can hear...' or 'I can see...' to practice speaking.
7. Give a big high-five to celebrate waking up your 'Super Senses'!

**Background Info**:
- Wetlands are areas where water covers the soil, or is present either at or near the surface of the soil all year or for varying periods of time during the year.
- The WWT London Wetland Centre is a 105-acre urban oasis, home to over 180 bird species.
- Autumn in wetlands is a time of change. Many birds are migrating, and plants are changing color.
- Sensory activities (using sight, sound, smell, touch) are crucial for brain development in young children.
- How to explain this to your child: 'A wetland is like a big, watery playground for ducks and birds! It’s not deep like the sea, but it’s wet and muddy and full of life!'
- The air near wetlands often has a distinct earthy smell due to decaying plant matter, which is a vital part of the ecosystem's nutrient cycle.

**Teaching Tips**: Use hand gestures to represent 'see' (point to eyes), 'hear' (cup ear), and 'smell' (point to nose). | Keep instructions short and clear. 'Close your eyes. Listen.' | Provide the sentence frame: 'I can see a...' and help your child fill in the blank. (e.g., 'I can see a big tree!') | If your child is shy, share what you see or hear first to model the activity. | Use a clear signal, like a special clap or a raised hand, to get everyone's attention in the open space.

**Phase Supplies**: Explorer Vests (made at home), Sit-upon mat or small blanket

**Conversation**: What sound was the most surprising to you? | Why do you think the air smells this way here? | If you could ask a bird a question, what would it be?

### 2. EXPLORE (Part 1) (20-60 mins)
**Activity**: Nature Detective Walk [Science/Technology]

Let's go on a Nature Detective walk! Our mission is to look for clues that tell us about the animals and plants living here. We will use our magnifying glasses to look closely at leaves, bark, and tiny insects. We will also use a special technology tool, the 'Seek by iNaturalist' app on a phone, to help us identify the amazing things we find. This is a real scientific investigation! By walking slowly and observing carefully, we can discover the secret stories of the wetland. Remember, we are detectives, so we look with our eyes, not our hands, for anything we don't know.

**Steps**:
1. Give your child a magnifying glass. Show them how to hold it close to an object to make it look bigger.
2. Start walking slowly along a safe, designated path.
3. Challenge them to find three different kinds of leaves. 'Look! This one is pointy. This one is round.'
4. Find an interesting plant or insect. Use the 'Seek by iNaturalist' app to identify it together. 'Wow, the phone says this is a 'Common Darter' dragonfly!'
5. Look for animal tracks in the mud. 'Whose footprints could these be?'
6. Remind your child of the 'look but don't touch' rule for unknown plants and creatures.
7. Take photos of at least 5 different things you discover.

**Background Info**:
- Autumn leaves change color because they stop making chlorophyll (the green stuff) as the days get shorter and colder. This lets the yellow and orange colors, which were there all along, finally show through.
- How to explain this to your child: 'In summer, leaves wear a green coat called chlorophyll. In autumn, they take off their green coat, and you can see their real yellow and orange pajamas underneath!'
- Many insects, like ladybugs, look for safe places to hide for the winter, often under bark or in piles of leaves.
- The WWT London is home to creatures like dragonflies, water voles, and newts.
- 'Seek by iNaturalist' is a free app that uses image recognition to identify plants and animals from photos.
- Looking for animal tracks (a science called ichnology) is a real method scientists use to study animal behavior without ever seeing the animal.

**Teaching Tips**: Pre-load the 'Seek' app on your phone before the workshop. | Model curiosity: 'Ooh, I wonder what this is? Let's check our special tool!' | Use comparative language: 'This leaf is bigger than that leaf.' 'This rock is smooth.' | Assign roles for the walk: one person is the 'Lead Detective' (looks ahead), one is the 'Photographer'. Switch roles halfway. | For children who need more focus, give them a specific mission: 'Your job is to find 5 red leaves.'

**Phase Supplies**: Magnifying glass, Smartphone with 'Seek by iNaturalist' app installed, Camera or phone camera

**Conversation**: Why do you think this leaf has holes in it? | If you were a tiny bug, where would you hide on this tree? | What do you think the app will say this flower is?

### 3. EXPLORE (Part 2) (60-90 mins)
**Activity**: Autumn Treasure Hunt & Sort [Science/Math]

Every great explorer collects treasures! In this part of our mission, we will have a treasure hunt for fallen natural items. We are looking for things that are already on the ground, like colorful leaves, interesting twigs, and smooth pebbles. We will use our collection jars to keep them safe. After our hunt, we will become mathematicians! We will sit down, empty our jars, and sort our treasures. How many red leaves did we find? How many yellow? We can make a simple graph on our paper to show our results. This is how scientists organize what they find!

**Steps**:
1. Give your child a collection jar or bag.
2. Explain the rules: 'We only collect things that have already fallen on the ground. We don't pick living plants.'
3. Set a timer for 10 minutes and let the treasure hunt begin! 'Ready, set, go!'
4. Help your child find different items: leaves of different colors, small twigs, pebbles, fallen seeds.
5. After the hunt, find a spot to sit and empty the treasures onto a piece of paper or tray.
6. Guide them to sort the items into groups. 'Let's put all the red leaves here. All the twigs here.'
7. Count each group together. 'One, two, three... three red leaves!' Draw a simple bar chart on a notebook page to represent the counts.

**Background Info**:
- Sorting and classifying objects is a fundamental mathematical skill, helping children recognize patterns and relationships.
- A simple bar chart is a visual way to represent data, making it easy to compare quantities.
- Decomposition is a key process in a wetland ecosystem. The fallen leaves and twigs we collect will eventually break down and provide nutrients for new plants to grow.
- How to explain this to your child: 'When leaves fall, they become food for the soil! It's like recycling, but for nature.'
- Many birds, like robins, use small twigs to build their nests.
- The different colors of autumn leaves are caused by pigments like carotenoids (yellows/oranges) and anthocyanins (reds/purples).

**Teaching Tips**: Use a visual checklist or picture card of items to hunt for (e.g., a picture of a red leaf, a brown twig). | Practice counting out loud together. Emphasize the last number: 'We have FIVE yellow leaves!' | For the graph, use simple squares. 'For every red leaf, we color one red square.' | Use sentence frames for comparison: 'We have MORE yellow leaves than red leaves.' | Ensure the collection area is safe and free from hazards like poison ivy or sharp litter. Define a clear boundary.

**Phase Supplies**: Collection jars or small bags, Notebook or paper, Crayons or colored pencils

**Conversation**: Which treasure is your favorite? Why? | Which color leaf did we find the most of? | What else could we sort these by? (e.g., size, shape, texture)

### 4. EXPLAIN (90-120 mins)
**Activity**: Animal Homes & Snack Time [Science]

After all that exploring, it's time for a rest and a snack! While we eat, let's talk about what we've discovered. Where do all the animals we saw live? We'll look at the photos we took and talk about animal homes. Ducks have nests near the water, bugs hide under leaves, and squirrels live in trees. We'll share some 'Did you know...?' fun facts about the animals at the WWT London Wetland Centre. This phase connects our hands-on exploration with scientific concepts, helping your child understand the 'why' behind what they saw.

**Steps**:
1. Find a comfortable spot for a snack break. Make sure to wash hands first!
2. Look through the photos you took on your phone during the Nature Detective walk.
3. Ask your child, 'Where do you think this duck lives? What does it eat?'
4. Share a simple fun fact. 'Did you know that a mother duck is called a hen?'
5. Talk about the treasures you collected. 'A little bug could use this big leaf as an umbrella!'
6. Introduce the word 'habitat'. 'A habitat is an animal's home. The wetland is a habitat for many animals.'
7. Have your child repeat the new words: 'Habitat', 'Nest'.

**Background Info**:
- A habitat is a natural environment where an organism lives, providing food, water, shelter, and space.
- Wetlands are crucial habitats. They act as nurseries for many species of fish and birds.
- Water voles, a protected species in the UK, create burrows in the banks of rivers and ponds.
- How to explain this to your child: 'A habitat is like your house. You have a kitchen for food, a bed for shelter, and a living room for space. An animal's habitat has all of that too!'
- Many birds at WWT London, like the Gadwall duck, are migratory, meaning they travel long distances between their summer and winter habitats.
- Insects are ectothermic (cold-blooded), so they rely on their habitat for shelter from extreme temperatures.

**Teaching Tips**: Use snack time as a natural, relaxed moment for conversation and learning. | Use the photos as visual aids to help your child remember and talk about their discoveries. | Provide the sentence frame: 'A [duck] lives in a [nest].' | Use gestures and actions. Flap your arms like a bird, or wiggle your fingers like a bug. | Simplify concepts. Instead of 'decomposition', say 'The leaves become food for the ground.'

**Phase Supplies**: Snacks and water, Hand sanitizer or wipes, Phone with photos from the walk

**Conversation**: If you could build a home for a frog, what would it look like? | Why do you think it's important to keep animal homes clean? | What's the coziest-looking animal home we saw today?

### 5. ELABORATE (120-160 mins)
**Activity**: Mini Bug Hotel Construction [Engineering/Art]

Now that we know how important homes are for animals, let's become engineers and build one! We are going to make a 'Mini Bug Hotel' using the natural treasures we collected. A bug hotel is a safe place for tiny creatures like ladybugs and beetles to rest and hide. Using a small cardboard box or plastic bottle, we will design and build a cozy home. We'll fill it with twigs, leaves, and pinecones to create lots of little rooms. This activity lets your child apply what they've learned about habitats in a creative, hands-on way.

**Steps**:
1. Give your child the empty plastic bottle or cardboard box. This is the hotel frame.
2. Show them how to carefully place the collected treasures inside. 'Let's put the twigs in first. Then the leaves.'
3. Encourage them to think like a bug. 'Would a ladybug like this little space? Is it cozy?'
4. Use the string to tie a little handle on top, so you can hang it in your garden or on a balcony at home.
5. Talk about the different 'rooms' they are creating with the materials.
6. Help them name their hotel. 'The Grand Bug Hotel!' or 'The Ladybug Inn'.
7. Take a picture of your child proudly holding their creation.

**Background Info**:
- Bug hotels, also called insect hotels, provide shelter for beneficial insects, especially pollinators like solitary bees and wasps.
- How to explain this to your child: 'We are building a little apartment building for bugs! Each hole and leaf is a different room for a different bug family.'
- Good materials for bug hotels include hollow stems (like bamboo), pinecones, rolled-up cardboard, and dry leaves.
- Providing shelter helps insect populations thrive, which is important for pollinating plants and controlling pests in gardens.
- This activity is a form of 'biomimicry' - learning from and mimicking strategies found in nature. We are copying how bugs find homes in logs and leaf piles.
- Engineering for kids involves a simple design-build-test cycle. Here, they design the layout, build the hotel, and will 'test' it by seeing if any bugs move in at home.

**Teaching Tips**: Focus on the process, not the perfect outcome. It's about creativity and exploration. | Use positional language: 'Put the leaf ON TOP of the twig.' 'Push the pinecone INSIDE the bottle.' | Let your child take the lead in design choices. 'Where do you think this big leaf should go?' | If working with a plastic bottle, an adult should pre-cut the opening for safety. | Connect the activity back to the theme of conservation: 'By building this home, we are helping the bugs.'

**Phase Supplies**: Empty plastic bottle (500ml) or small cardboard box, String or twine, Natural treasures collected earlier (twigs, leaves, pinecones, etc.)

**Conversation**: What kind of bug do you hope will move into your hotel first? | If you were a bug, which 'room' in the hotel would you choose? | What other materials from nature could we use to make a bug hotel?

### 6. EVALUATE (160-180 mins)
**Activity**: Explorer Show & Tell Circle [Science/Art]

Our mission is almost complete! It's time to gather for one last circle and celebrate our amazing discoveries. This is our Explorer Show & Tell. Each child will have a chance to show their favorite treasure they found or their amazing Bug Hotel. They can share one thing they learned today. What was the coolest thing you saw? What did you learn about wetlands? We will end by taking a group photo and awarding everyone their 'Wetland Protector' certificate. High-fives all around for a successful mission!

**Steps**:
1. Gather in a circle with your bug hotels and favorite treasures.
2. Go around the circle, giving each parent and child a chance to share.
3. Model the sharing first: 'My favorite treasure is this smooth stone. I learned that wetlands are important homes.'
4. Help your child share using a simple sentence: 'I found a red leaf.' or 'This is my bug hotel.'
5. Listen and cheer for everyone as they share. Give lots of positive encouragement!
6. Take a final group photo of all the proud Wetland Protectors.
7. Present your child with their 'Wetland Protector' certificate from the handbook.
8. Thank your child for being such a wonderful explorer today.

**Background Info**:
- Reflection is a key part of the learning process. It helps solidify new knowledge and connect it to personal experience.
- Show and Tell activities build confidence and public speaking skills, even at a simple level.
- Peer learning happens when children see and hear what their friends have discovered.
- Positive reinforcement, like certificates and praise, builds a love for learning and a sense of accomplishment.
- How to explain this to your child: 'We're having a party to show everyone our favorite things! It's a way to say 'Good job!' to ourselves and our friends for being great explorers.'
- Ending an experience with a positive, celebratory ritual helps create lasting, happy memories associated with learning.

**Teaching Tips**: Don't force a shy child to speak. They can share by pointing to their favorite thing. | Provide the sentence frames: 'My favorite thing is...' and 'I learned that...' | Use non-verbal cues like clapping, thumbs-up, and big smiles to show appreciation. | Keep it brief and positive. The goal is celebration, not a formal test. | Make the certificate presentation feel special. Call their name and shake their hand.

**Phase Supplies**: Bug Hotels (made in previous phase), Favorite collected treasures, Wetland Protector Certificate (from handbook)

**Conversation**: What was the most fun part of our adventure today? | What is one thing you want to tell someone at home about our day? | Where should we go exploring next time?

### POST-CLASS ADVENTURE (Post-Workshop)
**Activity**: Backyard Bug Hotel Check-in [Science]

The adventure doesn't end here! Your mission continues at home. Find a safe, sheltered spot for your new Bug Hotel in your garden, on a balcony, or even near a window. Over the next week, you and your child can be scientists and observe your hotel. Check it once a day. Do you see any visitors? You can draw any bugs you see in a nature journal. This extends the learning and shows your child that science and discovery can happen anywhere, even right outside their own door!

**Steps**:
1. Together, choose the perfect spot for the bug hotel. It should be protected from heavy rain.
2. Hang it from a tree branch or place it on a ledge.
3. Explain that you will be 'bug scientists' for the week.
4. Once a day, visit the hotel together. 'Let's check for guests!'
5. Use a notebook to draw any bugs you see. 'Look, a little spider is visiting!'
6. Talk about why the bugs might like the hotel you built.
7. After a week, talk about your observations. Did you have many visitors?

**Background Info**:
- Long-term observation projects teach patience and scientific methodology.
- Keeping a nature journal encourages drawing, writing, and detailed observation.
- Common visitors to bug hotels in the UK include ladybugs, lacewings, solitary bees, and woodlice.
- This activity reinforces the concept of conservation and shows that even small actions can help wildlife.
- Observing the hotel at different times of day might reveal different visitors, introducing the concept of diurnal vs. nocturnal animals.

**Teaching Tips**: Don't be discouraged if no bugs move in right away. It can take time. The act of observing is the main goal. | Use a magnifying glass for your home observations too. | Help your child write or label their drawings in the journal. 'S-P-I-D-E-R'. | Take photos to document any visitors over the week.

**Phase Supplies**: Completed Bug Hotel, Notebook or paper (for Nature Journal), Pencil or crayons

**Conversation**: Why do you think this spot is a good home for our bug hotel? | If a bug could talk, what would it say about its new room? | What other things could we do in our garden to help animals?
