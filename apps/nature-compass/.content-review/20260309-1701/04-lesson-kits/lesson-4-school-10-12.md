# Nature Compass: The Autumn Expedition
**school** | Age 10-12

## Roadmap (6 phases)

### ENGAGE (0-15 min)
**Activity**: Mission Briefing & Sensory Warm-up [Science]

To begin our expedition, we gather as a team of 'Nature Detectives'. The instructor presents the mission narrative, sparking curiosity about the 'secret message' of the autumn forest. This phase activates prior knowledge by asking students what they already know about autumn. We then engage our senses with a simple '5-4-3-2-1' warm-up: name five things you see, four things you can feel, three things you can hear, two things you can smell, and one thing you can imagine tasting. This primes their observation skills and introduces key sensory vocabulary in a low-pressure, interactive way.

**Steps**:
1. Gather students in a circle and present the 'Nature Compass' mission narrative.
2. Ask open-ended questions: 'What happens to trees in autumn? What animals might we see today?'
3. Introduce the '5-4-3-2-1' sensory warm-up game. Model the first step clearly.
4. Guide students through the game, encouraging them to point to objects as they name them.
5. Introduce the concept of being a 'Nature Detective' and explain they will get a special handbook to record their findings.
6. Distribute handbooks and collection bags, building excitement for the exploration ahead.
7. Briefly review the primary safety rule: 'Stay within sight of the instructors at all times.'

**Background Info**:
- Autumn, or Fall, is one of the four temperate seasons. It marks the transition from summer to winter.
- The most noticeable sign of autumn is the changing color of leaves on deciduous trees.
- This color change happens because trees stop producing chlorophyll, the green pigment used for photosynthesis.
- As the green fades, other pigments like carotenoids (yellows, oranges) and anthocyanins (reds, purples) become visible.
- Days become shorter in autumn, which is the primary trigger for trees to begin shutting down for winter.
- Many animals, like squirrels, begin gathering and storing food for the winter, a behavior called 'caching'.

**Teaching Tips**: Use expressive gestures and a mysterious tone of voice to deliver the mission narrative and hook the students. | For the sensory warm-up, provide sentence frames on a whiteboard: 'I can see a ____.', 'I can hear a ____.' | Pair students up to share their sensory observations before sharing with the whole group. | Use a special 'call and response' signal to get attention in the outdoor space, like 'Nature Detectives!' with the response 'On the case!'.

**Phase Supplies**: Student handbooks, Pencils, Small collection bags, Portable whiteboard/chalkboard

**Conversation**: What is your favorite thing about autumn? | Why do you think leaves change color? | What sounds do you hear right now in the park?

### EXPLORE (Part 1) (15-55 min)
**Activity**: Guided Nature Walk & Scavenger Hunt [Science/Art]

Now that our senses are sharp, we embark on a guided walk. Students use a visual scavenger hunt list in their handbooks to find specific natural items: a rough piece of bark, a smooth stone, a fallen leaf with more than one color, a feather, and something a squirrel might eat. This is a hands-on, kinesthetic activity where students actively search and observe their surroundings. The focus is on exploration and discovery, not formal identification. They are encouraged to touch, smell (safely), and describe the textures and shapes they find, building a tactile connection to the environment.

**Steps**:
1. Explain the rules of the scavenger hunt, pointing to the visual guide in their handbooks.
2. Define the physical boundaries for the hunt (e.g., 'Stay between this path and that large oak tree.').
3. Model finding the first item, thinking aloud: 'I am looking for rough bark. This tree feels bumpy and rough. I found it!'
4. Release students in pairs or small groups to search for the items.
5. Circulate among the groups, asking questions like 'How does that feel?' or 'What colors do you see on that leaf?'
6. Encourage students to place their found items gently into their collection bags.
7. Use the attention signal to gather the group periodically to share exciting finds.

**Background Info**:
- Tree bark has different textures for protection. Smooth bark is common on young trees like beech, while deeply grooved bark is found on older trees like oaks.
- The items squirrels eat are often nuts and seeds, like acorns from oak trees or chestnuts. These are high in fat and energy.
- Feathers have a complex structure with a central shaft (rachis) and barbs branching off, designed for flight and insulation.
- Stones are smoothed by the action of water (in rivers) or wind over thousands of years, a process called erosion.
- Leaf color variation can be caused by sun exposure, genetics, and the specific mix of pigments in the leaf.

**Teaching Tips**: Pre-walk the area to ensure the scavenger hunt items are available and the area is safe. | Assign roles within groups: a 'Looker', a 'Collector', and a 'Reporter' who will share the findings. | Scaffold language by providing a word bank of textures: 'rough', 'smooth', 'bumpy', 'soft', 'hard'. | For students who find items quickly, provide an extension challenge: 'Can you find three different kinds of leaves?'

**Phase Supplies**: Student handbooks with scavenger hunt page, Collection bags, Magnifying glasses

**Conversation**: Which item was the hardest to find? Why? | Describe the texture of the stone you found. | What do you think made this mark on the bark?

### EXPLORE (Part 2) (55-85 min)
**Activity**: Specimen Analysis & Digital Identification [Science/Technology]

In this phase, we transition from broad exploration to focused analysis. Students take out the leaves they collected and use magnifying glasses to observe the details, such as veins and edges. This is where we integrate technology. Using a tablet with a plant identification app (like 'Seek' by iNaturalist), the instructor helps students identify one or two common leaf types, such as Oak or Maple. This introduces them to how technology can assist scientific observation, bridging the gap between nature and the digital world. Students record the leaf name in their handbook next to a rubbing or drawing.

**Steps**:
1. Gather students and have them lay out their collected leaves on a provided mat or cloth.
2. Distribute magnifying glasses and instruct students to look closely at the leaf veins and edges.
3. Introduce the plant identification app on a tablet. Explain that it's a 'digital field guide'.
4. Choose a common, easily identifiable leaf from a student's collection (e.g., an Oak leaf).
5. Hold the tablet and model how to use the app to scan and identify the leaf. Announce the result clearly.
6. Have students find a similar leaf in their collection and repeat the name: 'This is an Oak leaf.'
7. Guide students to the correct page in their handbook to record their finding by making a leaf rubbing or drawing.
8. Repeat the process for one other common leaf type if time allows.

**Background Info**:
- Plant identification apps use artificial intelligence and computer vision to analyze photos and match them against a database of species.
- Leaf veins are like tiny pipes that transport water and nutrients. The pattern of veins (venation) is a key feature for identification.
- Oak leaves are known for their lobed (rounded) edges, while Maple leaves have pointed lobes and a palmate shape (like a hand).
- Richmond Park is famous for its ancient oak trees, some of which are over 750 years old.
- Leaf rubbing is a simple scientific illustration technique that captures the texture and vein pattern of a specimen.

**Teaching Tips**: Ensure the app is pre-loaded and tested in the park location, as connectivity can be an issue. | Focus on the process of using technology for discovery, not on identifying every single item. | Provide sentence frames for describing leaves: 'My leaf has ____ edges.' (e.g., 'pointy', 'rounded'). | Have pre-printed labels with the names of common trees (Oak, Maple, Beech) for students who struggle with writing.

**Phase Supplies**: Magnifying glasses, Collected leaves, Tablet with a pre-installed plant identification app (e.g., Seek by iNaturalist), Paper and crayons for leaf rubbings, Ground mats or blankets

**Conversation**: What did you see with the magnifying glass that you couldn't see before? | How is technology helpful for a scientist? | Are the veins on the leaf straight or branched?

### EXPLAIN (85-110 min)
**Activity**: Ecosystem Circle & Vocabulary Match [Science]

With concrete examples from their exploration, we now formalize the key concepts. The instructor leads a discussion to define the word 'ecosystem', explaining it as a community of living things (plants, animals) and non-living things (sun, soil, water) that work together. We introduce the roles within the ecosystem: producers (plants), consumers (animals), and decomposers (fungi, bacteria). Students participate in a vocabulary matching game, connecting pictures of their found items to these new terms. For example, a leaf connects to 'producer' and a worm or mushroom to 'decomposer'. This makes abstract scientific terms tangible and memorable.

**Steps**:
1. Gather students in a circle with their collection bags.
2. Introduce the word 'Ecosystem' [AUDIO_ICON] and provide a simple, A2-level definition.
3. Use a large diagram or felt board to show the sun, a plant (producer), an animal (consumer), and a mushroom (decomposer).
4. Explain each role simply: 'Producers make their own food. Consumers eat other things. Decomposers recycle dead things.'
5. Hold up examples from nature (a green leaf for producer, an acorn for consumer food).
6. Play the vocabulary match game: give students cards with the words 'Producer', 'Consumer', 'Decomposer' and have them place them next to a picture or real object.
7. Review the answers as a group, having students repeat the new vocabulary in a sentence: 'A tree is a producer.'

**Background Info**:
- An ecosystem is a biological community of interacting organisms and their physical environment.
- Producers, or autotrophs, create their own food, usually through photosynthesis. In this park, the main producers are trees, grasses, and shrubs.
- Consumers, or heterotrophs, cannot make their own food and must eat other organisms. A deer eating grass is a primary consumer.
- Decomposers, like fungi and bacteria, break down dead organic material, returning essential nutrients to the soil. They are nature's recycling team.
- The energy in an ecosystem flows from the sun to producers, then to consumers. Decomposers complete the cycle.
- Richmond Park is a vital ecosystem in London, designated as a National Nature Reserve and a Site of Special Scientific Interest.

**Teaching Tips**: Use physical movements to represent the vocabulary: stretch arms up like a plant for 'producer', pretend to eat for 'consumer', and wiggle fingers on the ground for 'decomposer'. | Keep definitions short and clear. Use analogies, like 'Decomposers are like the clean-up crew of the forest.' | Use a 'Think-Pair-Share' strategy for the matching game to encourage peer learning and reduce anxiety. | Have a 'Vocabulary Wall' on a portable whiteboard with the new words and pictures.

**Phase Supplies**: Large diagram/felt board of an ecosystem, Vocabulary cards (Producer, Consumer, Decomposer), Picture cards of plants, animals, fungi, Portable whiteboard and markers

**Conversation**: Is a squirrel a producer or a consumer? Why? | What would happen if there were no decomposers? | Are humans part of an ecosystem?

### ELABORATE (110-150 min)
**Activity**: Autumn Leaf Math & Nature Art [Science/Math/Art]

This phase challenges students to apply their knowledge creatively. First, we integrate mathematics. Students select three different leaves from their collection. They measure the length of each leaf using a simple ruler, ordering them from shortest to longest and recording the data. They then create a simple bar graph in their handbooks comparing the lengths. This activity reinforces measurement and data visualization skills. Following the math component, students use their collected leaves, twigs, and stones to create a temporary piece of 'Land Art' on a large sheet of paper, forming a picture of an animal or a pattern. This encourages creative expression using natural materials.

**Steps**:
1. Introduce the 'Leaf Math' challenge. Distribute simple rulers.
2. Model how to measure a leaf from the tip to the bottom of the stem.
3. Instruct students to choose three leaves, measure them, and record the lengths in their handbook.
4. Show them how to fill in a simple bar graph to compare the sizes.
5. Circulate to assist with using the ruler and creating the graph.
6. Next, introduce the 'Nature Art' activity. Lay out large sheets of construction paper.
7. Encourage students to arrange their collected natural items on the paper to create a picture or pattern, without using glue, emphasizing that this art is temporary.
8. Allow time for creativity and then host a mini 'gallery walk' for students to see each other's artwork.

**Background Info**:
- Mathematics is a fundamental tool in biology. Scientists use it to measure growth, population sizes, and analyze data.
- A bar graph is a chart that presents categorical data with rectangular bars with heights or lengths proportional to the values that they represent.
- Leaf size (length and surface area) is related to a tree's strategy for capturing sunlight. Large leaves are common in shady understories, while small leaves are common in dry, sunny climates.
- 'Land Art' or 'Earth Art' is an art movement in which landscape and the work of art are inextricably linked. It often uses natural materials.
- Artists like Andy Goldsworthy are famous for creating ephemeral sculptures from natural materials like leaves, stones, and ice.
- This type of art fosters an appreciation for the beauty of natural forms and textures.

**Teaching Tips**: Provide rulers with clear centimeter markings. Pre-teach or review numbers 1-20. | Use the sentence frame for the math activity: 'This leaf is ____ centimeters long.' | For the art activity, provide prompts if students are stuck, such as 'Can you make a spiral?' or 'Can you make the face of an animal?' | Take photos of the land art, as it is temporary. This can be shared with parents later. | Differentiation: For students needing support, provide pre-drawn axes for the graph. For advanced students, ask them to calculate the difference in length between the longest and shortest leaf.

**Phase Supplies**: Collected leaves and other natural items, Rulers (cm), Student handbooks with graphing page, Pencils, Large sheets of construction paper or cardboard

### EVALUATE (150-180 min)
**Activity**: My Nature Map & Show-and-Tell [Science/Art/Engineering]

For the final phase, students synthesize their learning by creating a 'Nature Map'. This is not a geographical map, but a conceptual one. On a designated page in their handbook, they draw the key things they discovered: the oak tree where they found acorns, the area with the smoothest stones, and the different leaves they identified. They must label at least three items using the new vocabulary. The evaluation is a 'Show-and-Tell' circle where each student presents their map, explaining one thing they learned using a simple sentence frame. This serves as a portfolio assessment, celebrating their individual journey and reinforcing the day's concepts.

**Steps**:
1. Explain the final task: creating a 'Nature Map' of their scientific journey today.
2. Show a simple example of a map, with drawings of a tree, a leaf, and a stone, and labels.
3. Instruct students to draw at least three important things they saw or found and to label them.
4. Provide colored pencils or crayons for the drawing.
5. Circulate and help students with spelling and ideas for their maps.
6. Once maps are complete, gather the group in a final circle for the 'Show-and-Tell'.
7. Provide the sentence frame: 'On my map, I have a ____. I learned that it is a ____.' (e.g., '...an oak leaf. I learned that it is a producer.').
8. Give each student a chance to share one thing from their map, offering applause and positive reinforcement.
9. Conclude by awarding a 'Junior Ecologist' certificate and reviewing the 'secret message' of the forest: that everything is connected.

**Background Info**:
- Mapping is a core skill in many scientific fields, including ecology, geology, and cartography.
- Conceptual maps, or mind maps, are diagrams used to visually organize information. They help in learning and memory recall.
- Show-and-Tell is a common pedagogical tool that helps young learners practice public speaking, listening skills, and summarizing information.
- Peer teaching and sharing reinforces learning for both the speaker and the audience.
- Celebrating achievements with certificates builds confidence and provides a sense of accomplishment, positively concluding the learning experience.

**Teaching Tips**: Manage time carefully to ensure everyone who wants to share has an opportunity. | Focus on effort and participation rather than artistic ability or perfect grammar. | Model the 'Show-and-Tell' sentence frame yourself first to show students what is expected. | This activity serves as a summative assessment. Use a simple checklist to note if students used key vocabulary and could recall a key concept. | End the workshop on a high note, praising their hard work as 'Nature Detectives'.

**Phase Supplies**: Student handbooks with map page, Colored pencils or crayons, 'Junior Ecologist' certificates

**Conversation**: What was the most interesting thing you put on your map? | If you came back to the park tomorrow, what would you look for? | What is one new word you learned today?
