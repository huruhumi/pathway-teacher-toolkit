# Nature Compass: The Wetland Protectors
**school** | Age 6-8

## Roadmap (6 phases)

### ENGAGE (00:00 - 00:15)
**Activity**: Wetland Protectors Mission Briefing [Science (Ecology), Art (Storytelling)]

Gather the young explorers for an exciting mission! 🚀 Start with a playful welcome song to build energy. Introduce the narrative of the 'Wetland Protectors' and their task to help the Piping Plover. Use a plush bird puppet to tell the story and show a picture of a real wetland. Ask simple questions like 'What is a home?' and 'What do animals need to live?' to activate their prior knowledge. This phase is all about creating excitement and a sense of purpose for the day's adventure, making them feel like real scientists on an important quest.

**Steps**:
1. Greet students with a high-energy welcome song like 'The Wheels on the Bus' but with nature words ('The birds in the trees go tweet, tweet, tweet...').
2. Introduce the Piping Plover puppet and the mission narrative in simple, dramatic terms.
3. Show a large, colorful map of the Jamaica Bay Wildlife Refuge, pointing out 'You are here!'
4. Ask engaging questions: 'Who lives in the water? Who flies in the sky?' and praise all answers.
5. Hand out 'Wetland Protector' name tags to each student to create a sense of identity.
6. Clearly state the goal: 'Today, we will find clues in nature and build a new home!'
7. Do a final 'Protectors, assemble!' cheer to transition to the next phase.

**Background Info**:
- Wetlands are areas where water covers the soil, or is present either at or near the surface of the soil all year or for varying periods of time during the year.
- Jamaica Bay Wildlife Refuge is a key stopover for migratory birds, with over 330 bird species recorded there.
- The Piping Plover (Charadrius melodus) is a small, sand-colored shorebird that is listed as a threatened species. They need safe, open, sandy areas near water to nest and find food.
- Wetlands act as natural filters, cleaning pollutants from water.
- They also protect coastal areas from flooding during storms by absorbing excess water like a giant sponge.
- Autumn is a crucial season for many animals in the wetland as they prepare for winter or migration.
- The water in wetlands can be saltwater, freshwater, or brackish (a mix of both).

**Teaching Tips**: Use lots of gestures, facial expressions, and vocal variety to convey meaning for A1 learners. | Keep language extremely simple. Use Total Physical Response (TPR): 'Everybody, stand up! March like a scientist!' | Provide the sentence frame for answering questions: 'I think a ___ lives here.' | For differentiation, have advanced learners try to name an animal they already know that lives near water. | Use a clear signal, like a special hand clap or a bird call sound, to get everyone's attention.

**Phase Supplies**: Piping Plover puppet, Large map of the park, Pre-made 'Wetland Protector' name tags, Portable speaker for music

### EXPLORE (Part 1) (00:15 - 00:55)
**Activity**: Sensory Nature Walk & I-Spy [Science (Observation, Biology), Math (Counting)]

Let's go on an adventure! Lead the students on a guided walk along a safe, designated trail. This is not about speed; it's about discovery. Encourage them to use all their senses: 'What do you see? What do you hear? What do you smell?' Guide them to look for different colors, shapes, and textures in nature. They will use their 'I-Spy' checklist from the handbook to find and count items like yellow leaves, smooth stones, and tall grass. This phase focuses on hands-on, direct observation of the environment, connecting the abstract mission to the real world around them. Remember to enforce the 'look but don't touch' rule for unknown plants and animals.

**Steps**:
1. Review safety rules before starting: 'Stay with the group! Walk, don't run! Look with your eyes!'
2. Hand out the 'Nature Walk I-Spy' page from their handbooks and a crayon.
3. Lead the group slowly down the trail, stopping frequently to point things out.
4. Model sensory observation: 'I see a red leaf! Let's count them. One, two, three!'
5. Use a 'listening minute' where everyone is silent for 60 seconds and then shares what they heard.
6. Encourage students to check off items on their I-Spy list as they find them.
7. Stay within the pre-defined safe zone, clearly marked for the children.

**Background Info**:
- Autumn leaves change color because trees stop producing chlorophyll (the green pigment) as days get shorter and colder.
- The dominant trees at Jamaica Bay include cottonwoods and black cherries, which will have yellow and reddish leaves in autumn.
- The tall grass often seen is Phragmites, an invasive species, but it provides cover for many birds.
- You might hear the call of a Northern Flicker or the chatter of sparrows.
- The air near a wetland often has an 'earthy' smell from decaying plant matter, which is a vital part of the nutrient cycle.
- Smooth stones are created by the constant motion of water (erosion) over long periods.
- Many insects, like dragonflies, are still active on sunny autumn days near the water.

**Teaching Tips**: Use sentence frames for sharing: 'I see a ___.' 'I hear a ___.' | Pair up students to help each other find items on the checklist. | Use a group management technique like a 'train' where students hold onto a rope to stay together on the trail. | Carry a 'discovery bag' to show pre-collected, safe examples of what they might find. | Praise effort and excitement over accuracy. The goal is engagement, not a perfect score on the I-Spy sheet.

**Phase Supplies**: Student handbooks, Crayons, First-aid kit, Long rope for the 'nature train', Whistle or attention-getting sound maker

### EXPLORE (Part 2) (00:55 - 01:25)
**Activity**: Nature Treasure Collection & Data Sorting [Science (Classification), Math (Sorting, Graphing), Technology (Digital Photography)]

Now it's time to become collectors! In a designated safe area, students will use collection jars to gather natural 'treasures' like fallen leaves, small twigs, and interesting pebbles. The rule is clear: only collect things that are already on the ground. After 10 minutes of collecting, bring everyone to a central spot with a large sorting mat. Students will empty their jars and work in small groups to sort their treasures by color, size, or type (leaves, stones, sticks). This phase introduces basic scientific classification and mathematical sorting in a tangible way. We will also use a tablet to take pictures of our collections, introducing a simple technology element.

**Steps**:
1. Establish a clear boundary for the collection area using colorful cones or flags.
2. Give each student a collection jar and demonstrate how to safely pick up items from the ground.
3. Set a timer for 10 minutes of collection time. Give 5-minute and 1-minute warnings.
4. Gather students on a large ground tarp or mat.
5. Instruct them to empty their jars and, as a group, sort the items into piles: 'All the leaves here. All the stones here.'
6. Guide them to sub-sort one category, for example, sorting leaves by color (yellow, brown, green).
7. Use a tablet or phone to take a 'data photo' of the sorted piles. Let students take turns pressing the button.
8. Ensure everyone washes their hands with sanitizer after handling natural materials.

**Background Info**:
- Classification is a fundamental scientific skill used to organize and understand the natural world.
- Sorting by attributes like color, shape, and texture are pre-mathematical skills that build a foundation for data analysis.
- The items collected represent the 'detritus' layer of the ecosystem, which is dead organic material that decomposes to create new soil.
- Even small pebbles can tell a story about the geology of the area; for example, quartz is common in this region.
- Using digital photography to record data is a common practice in field biology.
- This activity helps develop fine motor skills (picking up small objects) and teamwork (group sorting).
- The variety of items found can spark discussions about biodiversity, even on a small scale.

**Teaching Tips**: Model the sorting process clearly. Use simple labels for the sorting piles (pictures or words). | Use praise and encouragement constantly: 'Wow, what a beautiful yellow leaf you found!' | Assign roles in small groups: Sorter, Counter, Reporter. | For ESL learners, chant the names of the categories: 'Leaves, leaves, leaves! Stones, stones, stones!' | Use hand sanitizer immediately after the activity and before any snacks or moving to the next phase.

**Phase Supplies**: Small collection jars or bags for each student, Large tarp or sorting mat, Colorful cones or flags for boundaries, Tablet or smartphone for photos, Hand sanitizer

### EXPLAIN (01:25 - 01:45)
**Activity**: Nature's Clues & Vocabulary Circle [Science (Ecology)]

Let's talk about what we found! Gather the students in a circle on the grass for a snack and a focused learning session. This is where we formally introduce the vocabulary words related to the wetland. Using the items they collected, you will name and explain key concepts. For example, hold up a leaf and say 'This is a **leaf**. It is food for tiny bugs.' Hold up a stone and say 'This is a **stone**. It can be a home for a little crab.' We connect their hands-on discoveries to scientific language in a simple, repetitive, and context-rich way. This phase solidifies their understanding and gives them the words to describe their experience.

**Steps**:
1. Gather students in a circle and distribute water and a simple snack.
2. Use the collected 'treasures' as realia. Hold up one item at a time.
3. Introduce a new vocabulary word clearly: 'Leaf. Everybody say: leaf.'
4. Provide a simple, functional definition: 'A leaf helps the tree eat sunlight.'
5. Go around the circle and have each student point to or hold up the item you named.
6. Use vocabulary flashcards with pictures and words to reinforce the concepts.
7. Ask simple questions using the new words: 'Is this a leaf or a stone?'
8. Review all the new words at the end with a quick call-and-response game.

**Background Info**:
- The key vocabulary for this age group should be concrete nouns: wetland, leaf, stone, water, bird, home.
- A habitat is a home that gives an animal everything it needs to survive: food, water, shelter, and space.
- The concept of 'shelter' can be explained as a 'safe place' or 'house' for an animal.
- Food chains in a wetland are complex. A simple explanation is: 'Big fish eat little fish. Birds eat big fish.'
- Water is the most critical component of a wetland, defining the types of plants and animals that can live there.
- Jamaica Bay is an estuary, where freshwater from streams mixes with saltwater from the Atlantic Ocean.
- Decomposers (like bacteria and fungi) are nature's recyclers, breaking down dead leaves and animals to create nutrients for plants.

**Teaching Tips**: Use repetition constantly. Have students say the new words at least 3-5 times. | Connect words to actions (TPR): 'Show me the water. Wiggle like a fish in the water.' | Use a puppet to 'ask' the questions, which can be less intimidating for shy students. | Keep the energy up with a cheerful tone and lots of positive reinforcement. | Display the vocabulary cards on a portable board or on the ground in the middle of the circle for constant visual reference.

**Phase Supplies**: Water bottles and a simple, allergy-safe snack (e.g., apple slices), Collected nature items, Large vocabulary flashcards with pictures, Puppet

### ELABORATE (01:45 - 02:25)
**Activity**: Build a Mini-Wetland Model [Engineering (Design), Art (Sculpture), Science (Ecology)]

Now you are the designers! It's time to use everything we learned to build a perfect home for our Piping Plover friend. Each student or small group will get a tray and various materials (clay, sand, blue paper for water, and their collected nature treasures). Their challenge is to design and build a miniature wetland model. Guide them with questions: 'Where will the bird find water? Where is a safe place to rest? What will it eat?' This creative, hands-on project allows them to apply their new knowledge and vocabulary in a meaningful engineering and artistic challenge, solidifying their understanding of what makes a healthy habitat.

**Steps**:
1. Demonstrate how to create a simple model: lay down blue paper for water, use clay to make the land, add sand for a beach.
2. Distribute a tray and a kit of materials to each student or small group.
3. Encourage them to incorporate the 'treasures' they collected earlier (leaves, twigs, stones).
4. Circulate among the students, asking questions to prompt design thinking: 'Tell me about your wetland. What is this part?'
5. Provide small plastic animal figures (birds, fish, frogs) for them to place in their habitat.
6. Use the new vocabulary: 'I see you made a home with stones and leaves!'
7. Give a 5-minute warning for 'finishing touches' before cleanup.

**Background Info**:
- The design process (Ask, Imagine, Plan, Create, Improve) is a core component of engineering.
- This activity encourages spatial reasoning and problem-solving.
- Piping Plovers need wide, flat, sandy areas with little vegetation for nesting, so a good design would include a 'beach' area.
- They eat insects and small invertebrates found along the shoreline, so the 'water's edge' is a key feature.
- Adding twigs or small bits of grass provides shelter or camouflage for the bird.
- Building a model helps students synthesize information from different sources (observation, direct instruction) into a single creative output.
- This type of project-based learning is highly effective for language acquisition as it provides a clear, motivating context for communication.

**Teaching Tips**: Focus on the process, not the final product. Praise creativity and effort. | Provide sentence frames for describing their work: 'This is the water. This is a home for the bird.' | For students who struggle with fine motor skills, provide larger materials or assist them in shaping the clay. | Play calming nature sounds in the background to create a focused, creative atmosphere. | Take a photo of each student with their creation.

**Phase Supplies**: Individual trays (e.g., aluminum baking trays), Modeling clay (brown and green), Sand, Blue construction paper, Collected nature items, Small plastic animal figures (especially birds), Wet wipes for cleanup

### EVALUATE & WRAP-UP (02:25 - 03:00)
**Activity**: Wetland Gallery Walk & Certificate Ceremony [Science (Communication), Art (Presentation)]

Let's celebrate our amazing work! Arrange the mini-wetland models on the tables to create a 'gallery.' Students will then do a 'gallery walk' to see everyone's creations. Each student gets a chance to present their model, using the vocabulary they learned. Ask them simple questions like 'What is your favorite part?' and 'What animal lives here?' This serves as an informal assessment of their understanding. To conclude, hold a special 'Wetland Protector' certificate ceremony. Call each student's name, present them with their certificate, and end with a final group cheer, celebrating their successful mission and reinforcing their new identity as protectors of nature.

**Steps**:
1. Arrange the models on tables for the gallery walk.
2. Lead the students from one model to the next, giving each creator a moment to shine.
3. Encourage peer feedback with simple prompts like 'Say something you like about your friend's wetland.'
4. Use an observational checklist to note which students are using the target vocabulary correctly.
5. Gather everyone for the certificate ceremony.
6. Call each student's name individually to receive their 'Official Wetland Protector' certificate.
7. Lead a final cheer: 'We are Wetland Protectors! Hooray!'
8. Briefly mention the post-class activity: 'At home, you can draw a picture of your favorite wetland animal!'

**Background Info**:
- Peer-to-peer sharing (gallery walk) is a powerful tool for reinforcing learning and building communication confidence.
- Presenting one's own work, even in simple terms, is a form of summative assessment.
- Positive reinforcement through ceremonies and certificates builds self-esteem and a positive association with learning.
- Reflection is a key part of the learning cycle, helping to transfer knowledge from short-term to long-term memory.
- The post-class activity extends learning beyond the workshop and involves families.
- This final phase provides closure and celebrates the students' accomplishments, making the experience memorable.
- Conservation education aims to foster a sense of stewardship and responsibility for the environment from a young age.

**Teaching Tips**: Provide students with the sentence frame for their presentation: 'This is my wetland. A (bird) lives here. It has (water) and a (home).' | Use a microphone (even a toy one) to make the presentations feel special and fun. | Ensure every single student is praised for their work and effort. | Have parents/guardians gather for the last 10 minutes to watch the certificate ceremony, fostering a strong home-school connection. | End on a high, positive note with music and cheering.

**Phase Supplies**: Pre-printed 'Official Wetland Protector' certificates, Stickers or stamps for the certificates, Portable speaker for celebratory music, Observational assessment checklist for the teacher, Toy microphone (optional)
